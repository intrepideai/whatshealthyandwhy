Most people accept a prescription the way they accept a restaurant recommendation — with trust and without many questions. And in many cases, that trust is earned. But a prescription is not a dinner suggestion. It's a chemical intervention in your body's biochemistry, and you have the right — arguably the responsibility — to understand exactly what you're agreeing to.

Research shows that truly informed decision-making occurs in only about 9% of outpatient medical decisions [1]. That's not because doctors are withholding information maliciously. It's because the system isn't built for it — appointments are short, patient loads are heavy, and the path of least resistance is to prescribe and move on.

## Questions worth asking

These are not confrontational questions. They are the foundation of shared decision-making, which research identifies as the gold standard of patient-centered care [4]. Consider bringing them to your next appointment:

**What is the root cause of this problem?** Not just "what do I have" but "why do I have it." A diagnosis names the pattern. It doesn't always explain the origin.

**What lifestyle changes could address this?** Before or alongside medication, are there dietary changes, movement practices, sleep interventions, or stress management techniques that could help? If so, how much improvement could they realistically produce?

**What are ALL the side effects?** Not just the common ones in the pamphlet. What are the effects that show up at six months, two years, five years? What do patients actually report experiencing?

**How long will I be on this?** Is this a short-term intervention or a lifelong commitment? Is there a plan to reassess and potentially discontinue?

**What happens when I stop?** Will symptoms return? Is there a withdrawal process? Will I need to taper? Is there rebound risk?

**What nutrients does this medication deplete?** Many common drugs interfere with nutrient absorption or synthesis. Should I be supplementing? Should I be getting regular blood work to monitor levels?

**Are there evidence-based alternatives to try first?** Not instead of medicine if medicine is needed — but before defaulting to a pharmaceutical approach for a problem that might respond to other interventions.

## The case for shared decision-making

The concept of shared decision-making — where clinician expertise and patient values both shape the treatment plan — has strong support in medical literature [2][4]. It doesn't mean rejecting your doctor's recommendations. It means participating in them as an informed partner rather than a passive recipient.

This matters because your doctor, however skilled, does not live in your body. They don't know how a side effect feels to *you*. They may not know your priorities — whether you'd rather tolerate some symptoms than risk certain side effects, or whether you're willing to make significant lifestyle changes if it means avoiding medication.

## Functional medicine and root-cause thinking

Functional medicine offers an alternative framework that many people find valuable [3]. Rather than asking "what drug matches this diagnosis," it asks "what underlying imbalances are producing these symptoms?" It looks at the body as an interconnected system rather than a collection of separate organ specialties.

This approach is not without its critics, and it is not a replacement for conventional medicine when conventional medicine is needed. But the core principle — identify and address root causes rather than suppressing symptoms — is sound science, even if the conventional system isn't always structured to practice it.

## Second opinions are not disloyalty

If a mechanic told you your car needed a $4,000 repair, you'd get a second opinion. Yet people feel guilty about seeking a second medical opinion for decisions that affect their actual body. This is worth examining.

A second opinion is not an insult to your doctor. It's due diligence. Good doctors welcome it. If a recommendation is sound, a second opinion will confirm it. If it isn't, a second opinion might save you from an unnecessary intervention.

## A note on what this is — and isn't

This is not medical advice. We are not doctors, and nothing on this site should be used to self-diagnose or self-treat. This is not a suggestion to stop taking prescribed medication without medical supervision — doing so can be dangerous.

What this *is* — is encouragement to be an active participant in your own healthcare. To ask questions. To read the research. To understand what you're putting in your body and why. To seek practitioners who welcome your questions rather than dismiss them.

You are the only person who will be with your body for your entire life. It is not unreasonable to want the full picture before making decisions about it.
