Raw honey is one of the oldest medicines on Earth, used for wound healing and infection treatment for thousands of years across Egyptian, Greek, and Ayurvedic traditions. What sets raw honey apart from the clear golden liquid in a plastic bear is processing. Commercial honey is pasteurized at high temperatures, which destroys the enzymes, beneficial bacteria, and many of the bioactive compounds that give honey its medicinal properties. Raw honey retains its full arsenal: glucose oxidase (the enzyme that produces hydrogen peroxide), bee pollen, propolis, and a complex mix of antioxidants [1].

Honey's antibacterial power comes from multiple mechanisms working together. First, the enzyme glucose oxidase produces low levels of hydrogen peroxide when honey is diluted with wound fluids or moisture, creating a slow-release antiseptic effect. Second, honey's extremely low water activity (high sugar concentration) draws moisture out of bacteria through osmosis, effectively dehydrating them. Third, honey is naturally acidic with a pH between 3.2 and 4.5, which inhibits the growth of most pathogenic bacteria [1]. These overlapping mechanisms make it difficult for bacteria to develop resistance, unlike conventional antibiotics.

The FDA has actually approved medical-grade honey for wound treatment. Medihoney and similar products are used in clinical settings for burns, surgical wounds, diabetic ulcers, and pressure sores. Studies have demonstrated that honey-treated wounds show faster healing times, reduced infection rates, and less scarring compared to conventional dressings [5][3].

Manuka honey, produced by bees feeding on the Manuka bush (Leptospermum scoparium) native to New Zealand and Australia, takes antibacterial activity a step further. In addition to the standard mechanisms, Manuka honey contains methylglyoxal (MGO), a compound with potent non-peroxide antibacterial activity [4]. This means Manuka honey retains its antibacterial properties even when hydrogen peroxide activity is blocked, making it effective against a broader range of bacteria including antibiotic-resistant strains like MRSA.

UMF (Unique Manuka Factor) ratings measure the overall antibacterial potency of Manuka honey. A UMF of 10+ is considered therapeutically useful, 15+ is high-grade, and 20+ is exceptional. The MGO number indicates methylglyoxal concentration directly: MGO 100+ is minimum for therapeutic use, MGO 250+ is mid-range, and MGO 400+ is premium. When buying Manuka honey, look for the official UMF trademark from the UMF Honey Association, which verifies third-party testing.

As a cough suppressant, honey has been shown to outperform dextromethorphan (the active ingredient in most over-the-counter cough syrups) in children. A study at Penn State found that a single dose of buckwheat honey before bedtime reduced cough frequency and severity and improved sleep quality for both children and parents more effectively than DM or no treatment [2].

The claim that eating local honey relieves seasonal allergies is popular but has limited scientific support. The theory is that trace amounts of local pollen in raw honey could act like a natural immunotherapy. While this is biologically plausible, clinical trials have produced mixed results, and most allergists note that the wind-borne pollens causing most seasonal allergies are not the same pollens bees typically collect. It may help some people, but the evidence is not strong enough to make definitive claims.

One critical safety note: honey should never be given to children under 12 months of age due to the risk of infant botulism. The spores of Clostridium botulinum can be present in honey and, while harmless to older children and adults with mature digestive systems, can germinate in an infant's immature gut and produce dangerous toxins.

Mandal and Mandal (2011) published a comprehensive review of honey's medicinal properties in the Asian Pacific Journal of Tropical Biomedicine. They documented honey's activity against over 60 species of bacteria, including Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Escherichia coli. They established the three primary antibacterial mechanisms (hydrogen peroxide production, osmotic effect, and low pH) and noted that honey's complex composition makes bacterial resistance development unlikely. Their review also highlighted honey's anti-inflammatory properties mediated by suppression of NF-kB and reduction of prostaglandin synthesis [1].

Paul et al. (2007) conducted a randomized, partially double-blind study with 105 children aged 2-18 with upper respiratory infections. Children received a single dose of buckwheat honey, honey-flavored DM, or no treatment 30 minutes before bed. Honey consistently scored better than DM and no treatment for cough frequency, cough severity, bothersome nature of cough, and child and parent sleep quality. The superiority of honey over DM was statistically significant across multiple measures, leading the authors to recommend honey as a preferable treatment for childhood cough [2].

Jull et al. (2015) conducted a Cochrane systematic review examining honey for wound management across 26 randomized controlled trials with 3,011 participants. The evidence was strongest for partial-thickness burns, where honey-treated wounds healed 4-5 days faster than conventional dressings. For surgical site infections and chronic wounds including diabetic foot ulcers, honey dressings showed promising results including reduced bacterial colonization and improved granulation tissue formation, though the authors noted that study quality was variable [5].

Atrott and Henle (2009) characterized methylglyoxal as the key non-peroxide antibacterial compound in Manuka honey. They found MGO concentrations ranging from 38 to 761 mg/kg in commercial Manuka honeys, correlating directly with antibacterial potency. Importantly, MGO levels were stable during storage and were not destroyed by the catalase enzyme that degrades hydrogen peroxide, explaining why Manuka honey retains antibacterial activity even when peroxide-based mechanisms are neutralized. This unique chemistry distinguishes Manuka from all other honey varieties [4].
