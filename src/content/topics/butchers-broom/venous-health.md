Butcher's broom (Ruscus aculeatus) is a small evergreen shrub native to the Mediterranean whose root extract has been used for centuries to relieve heavy, aching legs and swollen ankles. Its active compounds — steroidal saponins called ruscogenins — work directly on vein walls, making them contract and tighten to keep blood from pooling in the lower limbs [4]. A large double-blind trial found that Butcher's broom extract significantly reduced leg volume and ankle circumference in women with chronic venous insufficiency over 12 weeks, with good tolerability [1]. Unlike many herbal remedies, this one has a clear mechanism and consistent clinical trial support across multiple countries.

## How Butcher's Broom Works

The root of Ruscus aculeatus contains two main active saponins: ruscogenin and neoruscogenin. These compounds act on the smooth muscle cells lining vein walls, stimulating alpha-adrenergic receptors to cause controlled vasoconstriction — tightening of the vein without constricting arteries [4]. The result is that veins hold less blood at any given moment, which reduces the hydrostatic pressure that pushes fluid out of capillaries and into surrounding tissue.

Butcher's broom also reduces capillary permeability, meaning the tiny blood vessels in the legs become less "leaky." In chronic venous insufficiency, elevated venous pressure causes capillaries to release inflammatory mediators and fluid into the interstitium — contributing to swelling, heaviness, and the dull aching that people feel after standing for hours. Ruscogenins appear to interrupt this inflammatory cascade at the capillary level [4].

A related benefit is postural: the venotonic effect helps the venous system act as an effective "return pump," reducing blood pooling when standing. This same mechanism may be useful for orthostatic hypotension — the dizzy drop in blood pressure that occurs on standing — where venous pooling in the legs reduces cardiac return [3].

## Conditions Where Evidence Exists

**Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI):** The primary use. CVI affects an estimated 25–40% of women and 10–20% of men, producing symptoms of leg heaviness, swelling, cramps, and varicose veins. Butcher's broom is approved by the European Medicines Agency for symptomatic relief of CVI [4].

**Hemorrhoids:** The same venous-tightening effect applies to the pelvic venous plexus. Butcher's broom is a common ingredient in European hemorrhoid treatments and has been used for this for over 200 years.

**Orthostatic hypotension:** The venotonic action reduces blood pooling on standing, potentially helping those who feel faint or dizzy upon rising from a seated or supine position [3].

## Dosage and Use

Typical dosing in clinical trials is 150–300 mg of standardized Ruscus aculeatus extract (standardized to 9–11% total saponins, including ruscogenins) taken two to three times daily with meals. The landmark RCT used a dose delivering about 36 mg total ruscogenins per day [1].

Butcher's broom is often formulated in combination with hesperidin methylchalcone (a bioflavonoid from citrus) and vitamin C. This combination appears in the well-studied European product Cyclo 3 Fort and has a long clinical track record dating back to the 1960s. The hesperidin adds capillary-strengthening effects and the vitamin C supports connective tissue synthesis in vein walls.

**Forms available:**
- Capsules or tablets of standardized extract
- Combination formulas with hesperidin and vitamin C
- Topical creams (limited penetration; less studied than oral)

**What to expect:** Effects tend to build over 4–8 weeks of consistent use. People most likely to benefit are those with visible leg swelling, varicose veins, feelings of heaviness after prolonged standing, or frequent leg cramps.

**Precautions:** Butcher's broom may raise blood pressure slightly due to its vasoconstrictive effects, and is generally not recommended for those with hypertension unless under medical supervision. Not well studied in pregnancy. May interact with alpha-adrenergic medications.

## Evidence Review

### Primary Randomized Controlled Trial

The most rigorous evidence for Butcher's broom comes from Vanscheidt et al. (2002), a multi-center, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial enrolling 166 women with chronic venous insufficiency (Widmer grade I–II, CEAP 3–4) [1]. The intent-to-treat analysis included 148 patients, divided evenly between Ruscus aculeatus extract (as Fagorutin Ruscus Kapseln) and placebo over 12 weeks.

Key outcomes at 12 weeks:
- Leg volume reduction in the ruscus group was significantly greater than placebo (-20.5 ml vs. a smaller reduction in controls; p < 0.05)
- Ankle and leg circumferences both decreased significantly in the treatment group
- Subjective symptoms including heaviness, tension, and discomfort all improved significantly more in the ruscus group
- Overall efficacy was rated as "good" or "very good" by significantly more patients in the treatment arm
- Adverse events were comparable between groups; only one adverse event in the ruscus group was possibly treatment-related

This trial was conducted to current standards with intent-to-treat analysis, validated measurement tools (water plethysmography for leg volume), and blinded assessment — making it the anchor study for Butcher's broom in the CVI literature.

### Capillaroscopic and Clinical Evaluation

Aguilar Peralta et al. (2007) conducted a prospective, multicenter open-label study of 124 ambulatory patients with CVI treated with the combination of Ruscus aculeatus, hesperidin methylchalcone, and ascorbic acid [2]. The study used nailfold capillaroscopy alongside standard clinical assessment — a more objective vascular endpoint than symptom questionnaires.

At baseline, symptom prevalence was high: 79% reported pain, 85% heaviness, 74% leg cramps, and 82% edema. After two weeks of treatment, these figures dropped dramatically to 20%, 12%, 8%, and 14% respectively. Capillaroscopic images showed normalization of microvascular architecture, including reduced perivascular edema and improved capillary loop regularity. While the open-label design limits causal inference, the speed and magnitude of improvement, alongside objective capillaroscopic changes, strengthens the clinical picture.

### Orthostatic Hypotension

Redman (2000) reviewed the pharmacological rationale and presented a case report of Butcher's broom use in orthostatic hypotension [3]. The patient, who had failed pharmacological interventions, demonstrated sustained improvement in orthostatic blood pressure response after beginning Ruscus aculeatus supplementation. The venotonic mechanism — reducing venous pooling in the lower limbs on standing — is mechanistically sound for this application, which represents an underexplored clinical use of this herb.

### Comprehensive Review

Allaert (2016) reviewed the full evidence base for the Ruscus + hesperidin methyl chalcone + ascorbic acid combination, spanning over 50 years of use in France and other European countries [4]. The review identified:

- Preclinical evidence demonstrating calcium-channel mediated and alpha-adrenergic venotonic effects
- Anti-inflammatory effects at the capillary level, including inhibition of neutrophil activation and reduced expression of inflammatory adhesion molecules
- Lymphatic effects: the combination has been shown to improve lymphatic contractility, relevant for the lymphedema component of advanced venous disease
- Multiple clinical trials showing reduction in leg circumference, symptoms, and quality of life impairment

The review concluded that this combination represents a well-characterized phytotherapeutic option for all stages of chronic venous disease, with a safety record spanning decades of European clinical use.

### Strength of Evidence

The evidence for Butcher's broom in chronic venous insufficiency is moderately strong for a botanical medicine: one well-conducted RCT, multiple supportive clinical studies, an established mechanistic explanation (ruscogenin/alpha-adrenergic pathway), and regulatory recognition by the European Medicines Agency. The main limitations are the relatively small number of high-quality RCTs, the predominance of European studies (which often used combination formulas making it harder to isolate Butcher's broom alone), and limited long-term safety data beyond 12 weeks. For hemorrhoids and orthostatic hypotension, the evidence is more preliminary, relying on mechanistic extrapolation and smaller studies.
