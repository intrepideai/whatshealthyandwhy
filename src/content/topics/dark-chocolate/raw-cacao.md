Raw cacao is chocolate in its least processed form — fermented and dried cacao beans, crushed into nibs or ground into powder, without the roasting and alkalizing steps used to make most commercial cocoa. That processing matters: Dutch-processed cocoa can lose 60–90% of its flavanols, the compounds responsible for most of cacao's documented health benefits [4]. A tablespoon of raw cacao powder or a small handful of nibs delivers a meaningful dose of epicatechin and other polyphenols — enough, based on clinical trial data, to influence cardiovascular function, memory, and mood [1][2][3].

## What Makes Raw Cacao Different

Cacao beans undergo natural fermentation after harvest — a process that develops flavor and converts harsh tannins into more bioavailable polyphenols. After fermentation, the beans are dried. At this stage, the cacao is still "raw" in the sense that it hasn't been roasted or chemically treated.

Most cocoa powder and chocolate go through two additional steps that strip flavanols:

- **Roasting** — high heat reduces flavanol content by 25–40%
- **Alkalization (Dutch processing)** — treating with an alkaline solution raises pH for a milder flavor but can destroy 60–90% of remaining flavanols [4]

Raw cacao powder and cacao nibs skip these steps, retaining a much higher concentration of epicatechin, catechin, and procyanidins — the polyphenols that drive most of the research findings on cocoa and health.

## Theobromine: Cacao's Unique Stimulant

Unlike coffee, cacao's primary stimulant is theobromine rather than caffeine. Theobromine is a milder, longer-acting methylxanthine that:

- Gently dilates blood vessels and bronchioles
- Provides a calm, sustained energy lift without the cortisol spike or crash associated with caffeine
- Has a half-life of 6–10 hours (vs. 3–5 for caffeine), so effects are gentler and more prolonged
- Contains mood-lifting properties independent of the flavanols [3]

Raw cacao also contains small amounts of phenylethylamine (PEA), a trace amine associated with focus and mild mood elevation, and is a notable plant-based source of magnesium — roughly 50 mg per tablespoon of powder.

## Practical Ways to Use Raw Cacao

**Cacao nibs** are the simplest form: crunchy, bitter-chocolate pieces that can be added to smoothies, oatmeal, yogurt, or eaten as a snack. They contain the whole cacao bean — fiber, fat, polyphenols — without added sugar.

**Raw cacao powder** (sometimes labeled "ceremonial grade") can be used in smoothies, hot cacao drinks, baked goods, or energy balls. Look for cold-pressed or non-dutched powder to preserve flavanol content.

**Cacao paste or mass** is ground whole cacao with fat intact, used in making real-food chocolate or ceremonially prepared drinks.

A typical research-effective dose is 400–900 mg of flavanols daily. For perspective, one tablespoon of high-quality raw cacao powder may provide 150–250 mg of flavanols — higher than the same amount of processed cocoa. The COSMOS trial used a standardized 500 mg flavanol extract [1].

Heavy metals are a real concern with cacao: lead and cadmium can accumulate in cacao beans depending on soil. See our [Flavanol Medicine page](/dark-chocolate/flavanol-medicine) for guidance on sourcing.

## Evidence Review

### Cardiovascular Health: The COSMOS Trial

The largest and most rigorous study of cocoa flavanols to date is the COSMOS (COcoa Supplement and Multivitamin Outcomes Study) randomized controlled trial [1]. The trial enrolled 21,442 US adults — 12,666 women aged 65 and older and 8,776 men aged 60 and older — randomized to 500 mg cocoa flavanols daily (including 80 mg epicatechin) or placebo for a median of 3.6 years.

The primary endpoint of total cardiovascular events showed a non-significant trend favoring flavanols (HR 0.90; 95% CI 0.78–1.02; P = 0.11). However, cardiovascular disease mortality — a pre-specified secondary endpoint — showed a statistically significant 27% reduction (HR 0.73; 95% CI 0.54–0.98). A per-protocol analysis accounting for adherence found HR 0.85 (95% CI 0.72–0.99), consistent with meaningful cardiovascular benefit when the supplement is actually taken regularly. The null result on diabetes risk (HR 1.04; 95% CI 0.91–1.20) in a separate COSMOS analysis [6] suggests benefits are more cardiovascular than metabolic in nature, at least at supplemental doses.

Earlier mechanistic work reviewed in Martin and Ramos [4] established that cocoa flavanols improve endothelial function, reduce LDL oxidation, lower platelet aggregation, and modestly decrease blood pressure — a plausible set of mechanisms for the observed reduction in CVD mortality.

### Memory and Cognitive Function

A 2023 study published in PNAS [2] examined whether dietary flavanol deficiency drives hippocampal-dependent cognitive decline in aging. The trial enrolled 3,562 older adults randomized to 500 mg cocoa flavanols or placebo for three years. Overall, the intervention did not significantly improve memory across all participants. However, among participants in the lower tertiles of habitual diet quality or baseline flavanol intake, flavanol supplementation significantly restored hippocampal-dependent memory performance — consistent with a depletion-repletion paradigm. Increases in urinary flavanol biomarkers over the trial period correlated directly with memory improvement. The implication: if you're already eating a high-flavanol diet (plenty of berries, apples, and cacao), the marginal benefit of a supplement is small. If your diet is low in these compounds, cacao flavanols may meaningfully protect age-related memory decline.

### Mood and Psychological Well-Being

A double-blind, placebo-controlled pilot RCT [3] gave 60 healthy Japanese women aged 40–60 either a flavanol-rich cacao extract beverage (240 mg flavanols/day) or placebo for 8 weeks. The cacao group showed significantly lower total mood disturbance scores, with reduced depression, fatigue, and anger subscores and significantly higher vigor compared to placebo (p < 0.05 for all). The mechanism likely involves flavanol effects on nitric oxide-mediated cerebral blood flow, theobromine's adenosine antagonism, and possible modulation of serotonin pathways via cacao's tryptophan content. As a pilot study with n=60, replication is needed, but the effect sizes were meaningful.

### Gut Microbiota

A 2024 review [5] synthesized clinical trial data and mechanistic studies on cocoa polyphenols and gut microbiota. Cocoa polyphenols consistently inhibit pathogenic bacteria such as Clostridium perfringens while promoting growth of Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species. The authors identified three microbial functional signatures associated with cocoa consumption: flavone/flavonol metabolism, aminobenzoate degradation, and fatty acid elongation. The bidirectional relationship — where gut bacteria also metabolize cocoa polyphenols into more bioavailable forms — helps explain why some trials show larger effects on markers like LDL and inflammation than flavanol content alone would predict.

A small crossover RCT (n=8) using high-dose supplementation (900 mg/day for 8 days) [reported in a 2024 secondary analysis] did not find significant changes in Bifidobacterium or Lactobacillus, and actually found lower Shannon diversity — a caution against very high acute doses and a reminder that longer-duration, moderate consumption is the pattern in positive studies.

### Strength of Evidence

The cardiovascular evidence is moderately strong: one large and well-designed RCT showing significant CVD mortality reduction, supported by mechanistic data. Memory benefits appear conditional on baseline flavanol status. Mood benefits are promising but need replication. The gut microbiota effects are biologically plausible and consistent across multiple study designs. Overall, raw cacao sits in a well-evidenced category of whole foods where the polyphenol content — preserved by minimal processing — is likely the primary driver of benefit.
