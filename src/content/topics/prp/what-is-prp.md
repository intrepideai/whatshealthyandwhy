PRP (platelet-rich plasma) is a concentrate made from your own blood that contains a high concentration of platelets and growth factors. It is used in orthopedics, dermatology, and aesthetics to promote tissue repair and regeneration.

PRP is not FDA-approved as a drug, but it is widely used as a medical procedure because it uses a patient's own biological material. FDA-cleared devices exist for preparing PRP, and the procedure is performed in clinics across the United States and worldwide.

## How PRP Is Prepared

The PRP process involves three basic steps:

1. **Blood draw** -- A small amount of blood (typically 15-60 mL) is drawn from the patient's arm, similar to a routine lab draw [1].
2. **Centrifugation** -- The blood is placed in a centrifuge and spun at high speed. This separates the blood into three layers: red blood cells at the bottom, a "buffy coat" of white blood cells and platelets in the middle, and platelet-poor plasma on top [3].
3. **Concentration and injection** -- The platelet-rich layer is extracted, resulting in a concentration of platelets 3-5 times higher than normal blood levels. This concentrate is then injected directly into the target area [1].

The entire preparation takes about 15-30 minutes and is typically done in-office.

## Growth Factors in PRP

Platelets contain alpha granules that release several key growth factors upon activation [4]:

- **PDGF (Platelet-Derived Growth Factor)** -- stimulates cell replication and blood vessel formation
- **TGF-beta (Transforming Growth Factor)** -- promotes collagen synthesis and tissue remodeling
- **VEGF (Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor)** -- stimulates new blood vessel growth
- **EGF (Epidermal Growth Factor)** -- promotes cell growth and differentiation
- **IGF-1 (Insulin-Like Growth Factor)** -- mediates cell growth and repair

These growth factors work together to accelerate the body's natural healing cascade [4].

## Common Applications

PRP is used across several medical fields:

- **Joint healing** -- Injected into damaged joints, tendons, and ligaments to reduce pain and promote tissue repair, particularly in knee osteoarthritis and rotator cuff injuries [1].
- **Hair restoration** -- Injected into the scalp to stimulate dormant hair follicles and promote hair growth in androgenetic alopecia [2].
- **Skin rejuvenation** -- Applied topically after microneedling or injected into the skin to stimulate collagen production and improve skin texture [2].

## Preparation Variables That Affect Outcomes

Not all PRP is created equal. Several variables in preparation significantly influence clinical results [4]:

- **Centrifuge speed and time** -- Different protocols produce different platelet concentrations. Single-spin protocols yield lower concentrations (2-3x baseline), while double-spin protocols can achieve 5-8x baseline [3].
- **Leukocyte content** -- PRP can be leukocyte-rich (LR-PRP) or leukocyte-poor (LP-PRP). LR-PRP contains white blood cells that may increase inflammation, which can be beneficial for tendon injuries but detrimental in joint spaces [4].
- **Activation method** -- Platelets can be activated with calcium chloride, thrombin, or simply by exposure to collagen at the injection site. The activation method affects growth factor release kinetics [1].
- **Volume and platelet count** -- There is no universal standard for the "ideal" platelet concentration. Studies use widely varying protocols, which contributes to inconsistent results across clinical trials [4].

Understanding these variables is important when evaluating PRP studies, as two trials using "PRP" may actually be using very different products [4].
