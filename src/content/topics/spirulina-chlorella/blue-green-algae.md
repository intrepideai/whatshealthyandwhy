Spirulina and chlorella are both microscopic, nutrient-dense organisms consumed as health supplements -- but they are fundamentally different. Spirulina (*Arthrospira platensis*) is a blue-green cyanobacterium, not technically an alga. Chlorella (*Chlorella vulgaris*) is a true single-celled green alga. Both are among the most nutrient-concentrated food sources on the planet, offering complete protein (containing all essential amino acids), B vitamins, iron, and a range of antioxidants [1][4].

NASA studied spirulina as a compact, high-yield food source for long-duration space missions, noting that 1 kg of spirulina has the nutritional equivalent of approximately 1,000 kg of assorted vegetables [5].

### Spirulina: The Phycocyanin Powerhouse

Spirulina's defining compound is phycocyanin -- the blue pigment protein responsible for its color and its most studied health effects. Phycocyanin has demonstrated significant antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity, including inhibition of COX-2, reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines, and scavenging of reactive oxygen species [3]. These properties set spirulina apart from most other plant-based supplements.

Spirulina is typically 60-70% protein by dry weight, making it one of the most protein-dense foods in existence [5]. It also contains significant gamma-linolenic acid (GLA), an omega-6 fatty acid with anti-inflammatory properties, along with iron, manganese, and B vitamins (though the B12 in spirulina is largely pseudovitamin B12 and not bioavailable to humans).

### Chlorella: Detox and Cell Repair

Chlorella's signature feature is its tough cell wall, which must be mechanically "broken" or "cracked" during processing to make its nutrients bioavailable [4]. This cell wall, rich in a complex called Chlorella Growth Factor (CGF), is what gives chlorella its unique detoxification properties. The cell wall binds to heavy metals like mercury, lead, and cadmium in the digestive tract, potentially aiding their excretion.

Chlorella is the richest known natural source of chlorophyll, containing 3-5% chlorophyll by dry weight -- roughly 10 times more than spirulina [4]. It also provides a strong amino acid profile, nucleic acids (RNA/DNA), and a broader vitamin profile than spirulina, including bioavailable B12.

When choosing between them, they serve complementary purposes: spirulina for anti-inflammatory and antioxidant support via phycocyanin, chlorella for detoxification and chlorophyll content.

### Phycocyanin: Mechanism of Action

Romay et al. (2003) characterized phycocyanin's anti-inflammatory pathway in detail. The compound inhibits cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2) activity with an IC50 of 180 nM, selectively targeting the inflammatory COX-2 isoform over the constitutive COX-1 [3]. This selectivity profile is notable because it mirrors the mechanism of pharmaceutical COX-2 inhibitors but without documented gastrointestinal side effects at normal doses.

Additional phycocyanin activities include:

- **Superoxide radical scavenging**: Direct neutralization of O2- radicals, protecting mitochondrial membrane integrity.
- **Peroxynitrite inhibition**: Blocks formation of the highly reactive nitrogen species ONOO-, reducing nitrosative stress.
- **Hepatoprotection**: Animal models show phycocyanin protects against CCl4-induced liver damage by reducing lipid peroxidation [3].

### Spirulina Nutritional Research

Deng and Chow (2010) reviewed spirulina's lipid-lowering and antioxidant properties, finding consistent reductions in total cholesterol, LDL, and triglycerides across multiple human trials, along with increases in HDL [2]. The hypolipidemic effect appears mediated by both phycocyanin and the GLA content, which modulates prostaglandin metabolism.

Karkos et al. (2011) summarized NASA's evaluation alongside clinical evidence, noting spirulina's unusually high bioavailability of iron and protein compared to other plant sources, and its established safety profile at doses up to 19 grams per day in clinical settings [5].

### Chlorella's Unique Properties

Merchant and Andre (2001) reviewed chlorella's multifunctional role, with particular emphasis on its heavy metal binding capacity. The fibrous cell wall material acts as an ion-exchange resin in the gut, binding divalent metal ions (Hg2+, Pb2+, Cd2+) and reducing their systemic absorption [4]. This mechanism is distinct from pharmaceutical chelation -- chlorella works in the GI tract before metals are absorbed, rather than pulling metals from tissues.

Additional findings include:

- **Immune modulation**: Chlorella supplementation (5 g/day) increased salivary IgA levels and natural killer cell activity in controlled trials.
- **Nucleic acid content**: Chlorella's high RNA/DNA content may support cellular repair processes, though human evidence for this specific claim remains limited.
- **Growth factor**: CGF contains peptides and polysaccharides that have shown wound-healing and growth-promoting properties in animal models [4].

### Safety and Quality

Both spirulina and chlorella have established safety profiles at typical supplemental doses (1-10 g/day) [1]. The primary safety concern is sourcing -- algae grown in uncontrolled or contaminated water can accumulate heavy metals, microcystins, and other toxins. Third-party tested products grown in controlled, closed-system environments are strongly preferred. Marles et al. (2011) found that the safety concerns attributed to spirulina in case reports were primarily traceable to contaminated products rather than spirulina itself [1].
