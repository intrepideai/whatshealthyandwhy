Beyond their raw nutritional density, spirulina and chlorella have specific functional properties that have attracted clinical research attention -- particularly around heavy metal detoxification, immune modulation, cholesterol reduction, and exercise performance. The evidence is stronger in some areas than others, and quality sourcing turns out to be just as important as dosing.

Start with a low dose (1-2 grams per day) and increase gradually. Both algae can cause digestive adjustment including gas, bloating, and changes in stool color during the first week.

### Heavy Metal Binding

Chlorella's primary claim to fame in the detox space is its capacity to bind heavy metals in the gastrointestinal tract. The broken cell wall material contains fibrous polysaccharides that act as ion-exchange resins, binding to mercury, lead, cadmium, and arsenic before they cross the intestinal barrier. This is a preventive mechanism -- it reduces absorption of metals from food and water rather than pulling stored metals from tissues.

Animal studies have demonstrated reduced tissue accumulation of mercury and cadmium with concurrent chlorella supplementation. In pregnant women, Nakano et al. (2010) found that chlorella supplementation (6 g/day) during pregnancy significantly reduced dioxin levels in breast milk and improved markers of anemia and proteinuria [4]. While this doesn't directly prove metal chelation in all populations, it supports chlorella's role in reducing toxic burden.

### Immune Function and Allergies

Spirulina has shown immunomodulatory effects across several trials. Cingi et al. (2008) conducted a double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in 127 patients with allergic rhinitis. Spirulina supplementation (2 g/day for 6 months) significantly reduced nasal discharge, sneezing, nasal congestion, and itching compared to placebo [3]. The mechanism appears related to spirulina's ability to inhibit histamine release from mast cells and suppress IgE-mediated immune responses.

Park et al. (2008) demonstrated that spirulina supplementation (8 g/day for 16 weeks) in elderly Koreans increased IL-2 production and enhanced immune cell activity, suggesting a role in supporting age-related immune decline [5].

### Powder vs. Tablets

Powder mixes into smoothies, juices, or water and offers dosing flexibility. Tablets are more convenient and avoid the strong taste (spirulina in particular has a distinctive marine flavor). Nutritionally, there is no meaningful difference if the tablet uses the same source material without excessive fillers. Pressed tablets may have slightly slower dissolution, but bioavailability studies have not shown clinically significant differences.

### Cholesterol and Metabolic Markers

Parikh et al. (2001) studied spirulina supplementation (2 g/day for 2 months) in 25 patients with type 2 diabetes. The treatment group showed significant reductions in fasting blood glucose, post-prandial blood glucose, and HbA1c. Lipid profiles also improved: total cholesterol decreased, LDL decreased, and the LDL-to-HDL ratio improved significantly [1]. The authors attributed the lipid effects to spirulina's GLA content and phycocyanin-mediated antioxidant activity.

These findings align with the broader literature reviewed by Deng and Chow, which found consistent hypolipidemic effects across multiple spirulina trials using doses of 1-8 g/day.

### Athletic Performance and Endurance

Kalafati et al. (2010) conducted a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover study examining spirulina's effects on exercise performance and oxidative stress in moderately trained men. Participants receiving 6 g/day of spirulina for 4 weeks showed significantly increased time to fatigue during a treadmill running test compared to placebo [2]. The spirulina group also demonstrated:

- **Reduced lipid peroxidation**: Lower malondialdehyde (MDA) levels after exercise, indicating less oxidative damage to cell membranes.
- **Increased glutathione (GSH)**: Higher resting and post-exercise GSH levels, suggesting enhanced endogenous antioxidant capacity.
- **Fat oxidation**: Higher rates of fat oxidation during submaximal exercise, with corresponding lower rates of carbohydrate oxidation [2].

The proposed mechanism is that phycocyanin's antioxidant capacity reduces exercise-induced oxidative stress, delaying fatigue onset and improving substrate utilization during prolonged activity.

### Chlorella in Pregnancy and Detox Contexts

Nakano et al. (2010) enrolled 70 pregnant women in a controlled trial of chlorella supplementation (6 g/day from weeks 12-18 through delivery). The chlorella group showed significantly lower rates of anemia (reduced by approximately 50%), lower proteinuria, and reduced edema compared to controls [4]. Dioxin concentrations in breast milk were approximately 30% lower in the supplemented group.

These findings are particularly relevant because pregnancy is a period of heightened vulnerability to environmental toxin transfer. The reduction in dioxin levels suggests chlorella's binding capacity extends beyond heavy metals to lipophilic organic pollutants.

### Quality Sourcing: A Non-Negotiable Factor

The single most important variable in algae supplementation is source quality. Spirulina and chlorella readily absorb whatever is in their growing environment -- this is the same property that makes chlorella useful for binding toxins, but it means contaminated growing conditions produce contaminated product. Key considerations:

- **Closed-system cultivation**: Algae grown in sealed photobioreactors or covered ponds avoid contamination from industrial runoff, agricultural chemicals, and wild cyanobacteria.
- **Third-party heavy metal testing**: Look for products tested for mercury, lead, arsenic, cadmium, and microcystins. ConsumerLab and NSF International provide independent testing.
- **Country of origin**: Products from countries with rigorous environmental standards (Japan, Australia, USA, parts of Europe) generally carry lower contamination risk.
- **Broken cell wall (chlorella only)**: Unprocessed chlorella has an indigestible cell wall. "Broken cell wall" or "cracked cell wall" processing is essential for nutrient absorption [4].
