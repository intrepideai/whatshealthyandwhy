Grounding, also called earthing, is the practice of making direct skin contact with the earth's surface — grass, soil, sand, or natural water. The idea is simple: walk barefoot outside.

The earth carries a mild negative electrical charge on its surface. When your bare skin touches the ground, free electrons transfer into your body. Proponents argue these electrons act as natural antioxidants, neutralizing positively charged free radicals that drive chronic inflammation.

## The electrical charge hypothesis

The earth maintains a negative surface charge replenished by lightning strikes, solar radiation, and atmospheric electricity. Modern life — rubber-soled shoes, insulated flooring, elevated buildings — has largely disconnected us from this charge. Chevalier, Sinatra, and Oschman proposed that this disconnection may contribute to the rise of chronic inflammatory conditions [1].

The hypothesis is straightforward: free electrons from the earth's surface enter the body through the skin, where they can neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS). ROS are necessary for immune function but become damaging in excess, driving the inflammatory cascade behind conditions from arthritis to cardiovascular disease [3].

## Cortisol and sleep

Ghaly and Teplitz conducted a controlled study where subjects slept grounded using conductive carbon fiber mattress pads connected to the earth. Grounded participants showed normalized cortisol secretion patterns — cortisol peaked appropriately in the morning and dropped at night, rather than remaining elevated. They also reported significant improvements in sleep quality, pain reduction, and stress levels [2].

## Inflammation and cardiovascular markers

Oschman et al. reviewed the evidence for grounding's effect on inflammation and immune response, finding that earthing appears to reduce markers of acute and chronic inflammation [3]. Separately, Chevalier and Sinatra demonstrated that just two hours of grounding significantly reduced blood viscosity — a key factor in cardiovascular disease — by reducing red blood cell clumping (zeta potential improvement) [4].

## The simplest starting point

None of this requires equipment. Walk barefoot on grass, soil, or sand for 20 to 30 minutes. That's the entire practice.

## Assessing the evidence

The grounding research base is still small and largely produced by a tight-knit group of researchers, several of whom have commercial interests in grounding products. Sample sizes tend to be small, and blinding is difficult — subjects generally know whether they're grounded or not.

That said, the mechanisms are biophysically plausible. Electron transfer through skin contact is measurable. Cortisol normalization and blood viscosity changes have been demonstrated under controlled conditions [1][2][4]. The inflammatory model proposed by Oschman et al. aligns with established understanding of ROS and antioxidant behavior [3].

The practice carries essentially zero risk and zero cost. Even if the electrical mechanism proves less significant than proposed, the act of spending time outdoors barefoot — contact with nature, light physical activity, stress reduction — has independent health value. The risk-benefit calculation heavily favors trying it.

What's needed: larger randomized controlled trials with proper blinding (sham grounding devices), longer durations, and researchers without commercial ties to grounding products.
