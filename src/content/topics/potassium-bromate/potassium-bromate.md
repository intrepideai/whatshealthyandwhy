Potassium bromate is a chemical added to flour and bread dough to make loaves rise higher, hold their shape, and develop a chewier crumb. It is classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer as a Group 2B "possibly carcinogenic to humans" agent, based on consistent animal evidence that it causes kidney tumors, mesotheliomas, and thyroid tumors at doses comparable to long-term human dietary exposure [1][7]. The European Union, United Kingdom, Canada, Brazil, China, India, Sri Lanka, Nigeria, and Peru have banned it in food. The United States has not — the FDA still permits it, relying on the assumption that proper baking destroys most of the residue. Multiple analytical studies show that assumption does not always hold, with bread sampled around the world testing well above the FDA's 0.02 µg/g limit [5][6]. California became the first U.S. state to ban it under AB 418, with the prohibition taking effect on January 1, 2027 [8].

## What Potassium Bromate Does in Bread

Potassium bromate (KBrO3) is what bakers call a "maturing agent" or "flour improver." Wheat flour contains gluten proteins that need to be cross-linked into a strong, elastic network to trap fermentation gas and produce a tall, fluffy loaf. Traditional milling and aging accomplish this naturally over weeks. Potassium bromate accomplishes the same thing in minutes — it is a strong oxidizer that snaps disulfide bonds between cysteine residues in gluten proteins and re-forms them into a tighter network. The result is a higher rise, finer crumb, and more uniform texture, all from a tiny dose of bromate added to the flour at 15-50 parts per million.

The theory behind permitting it as a food additive is that during baking, KBrO3 is reduced to potassium bromide (KBr), which is non-toxic. In a perfect world, every bromate ion would be converted, leaving zero residue. In practice, conversion is incomplete and depends on dough thickness, baking time, oven temperature, the presence of reducing agents, and the initial bromate concentration. Modern analytical chemistry can detect residual bromate down to roughly 0.5 ppb, and finds it routinely in commercial bread.

## Why It Causes Cancer in Animals

Potassium bromate is one of the cleanest examples of a chemical carcinogen acting through oxidative DNA damage. When KBrO3 enters cells in the presence of glutathione or cysteine — which are abundant in every cell — it generates reactive bromine species that strip an electron from guanine bases in DNA, producing 8-hydroxydeoxyguanosine (8-OHdG), a hallmark mutagenic lesion [3][4]. If 8-OHdG is not repaired before the cell divides, it pairs incorrectly during replication and locks in a permanent mutation.

The kidney is the primary target organ because bromate is excreted by the kidney and concentrates in the proximal tubules. Studies in male and female rats consistently show that 8-OHdG levels rise in kidney DNA after just a few days of bromate exposure, persist throughout the dosing period, and correlate with proximal tubule cell proliferation — the combination that produces renal cell adenomas and carcinomas over months [3]. The thyroid and peritoneal mesothelium are secondary targets, with the same oxidative-stress mechanism operating in both [4][7].

## The Regulatory Split

Most countries that have evaluated potassium bromate have banned it from food. The UK banned it in 1990. Canada banned it in 1994. The European Union banned it across all member states. China, India, Brazil, Sri Lanka, Nigeria, and Peru have banned it. JECFA, the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives, withdrew its earlier acceptable daily intake in 1992 and concluded that the use of potassium bromate as a flour treatment agent is "not appropriate" given the available data on carcinogenicity [7].

The United States is the prominent holdout. The FDA acknowledges the IARC classification but maintains that, when used as directed, properly baked bread contains residues below the limit of public-health concern. The agency has urged voluntary discontinuation since 1991 but has never moved to ban. Many large U.S. bakeries — including King Arthur, Pepperidge Farm, and most national bread brands — have voluntarily reformulated. But potassium bromate is still legal and still appears on ingredient lists of regional bakeries, fast-food bun suppliers, frozen pizza dough, and bagel chains.

California became the first U.S. jurisdiction to ban it through Assembly Bill 418, the California Food Safety Act, signed into law in October 2023. The ban prohibits the manufacture, sale, or distribution of any food containing potassium bromate (along with brominated vegetable oil, propylparaben, and Red Dye No. 3) starting January 1, 2027 [8]. Because California's market is large enough to drive national reformulation, AB 418 is expected to functionally end potassium bromate use in U.S. mass-market bread.

## How to Avoid It

The simplest defense is to read ingredient lists. "Potassium bromate" or "bromated flour" on a label means it is in the product. Common places to check:

- Hamburger and hot-dog buns at fast-food chains and grocery stores
- Frozen pizza dough and pizza-restaurant crusts
- Bagels, especially from large commercial chains
- Tortillas, naan, and pita from some manufacturers
- Pastry flour used in commercial baked goods

Buying bread certified organic eliminates it automatically: the USDA organic standard prohibits potassium bromate. Bread sold in California, the EU, UK, or Canada will not contain it. Sourdough and traditionally fermented breads also rarely contain it, since their long fermentation makes the dough-strengthening shortcut unnecessary.

If you bake at home, look for unbleached, unbromated flour. King Arthur, Bob's Red Mill, and most artisan flours are unbromated. The label often states "unbromated" explicitly.

## Evidence Review

The carcinogenicity of potassium bromate has been studied for over forty years across multiple species, dose ranges, and exposure routes. The evidence is unusually consistent for an animal carcinogen and the mechanism is well characterized — which is why most regulatory agencies outside the United States have concluded that human exposure should be eliminated rather than minimized.

**Kurokawa 1990 (PMID 2269236)** synthesized two decades of Japanese research on potassium bromate. In long-term studies, oral administration of KBrO3 in drinking water at 250 and 500 mg/L for 110 weeks produced renal cell tumors in 60-88% of male F344 rats and 42-60% of females, compared to background rates of 0-2% in controls. Mesotheliomas of the peritoneum appeared in 12-31% of treated males. Thyroid follicular cell tumors appeared in 9-15% of treated females. The authors classified KBrO3 as a complete carcinogen with both initiating and promoting activity in two-stage carcinogenesis assays. They also documented that the compound is genotoxic — strongly inducing chromosome aberrations both in vitro and in bone marrow micronucleus tests in vivo — but only weakly mutagenic in standard bacterial assays, a profile typical of oxidative-stress carcinogens.

**DeAngelo 1998 (PMID 9789944)** is the U.S. EPA's pivotal animal study. Male B6C3F1 mice received 0, 0.08, 0.4, or 0.8 g/L KBrO3 in drinking water for up to 100 weeks. Male F344/N rats received 0, 0.02, 0.1, 0.2, or 0.4 g/L for up to 100 weeks. Renal cell tumors increased in a dose-dependent fashion in both species, with statistically significant increases at the lowest tested doses (0.08 g/L in mice; 0.1 g/L in rats). Mesotheliomas of the peritoneum reached 64% incidence in the highest-dose male rats versus 4% in controls. The dose-response curve showed no clear threshold — every tested dose produced excess tumors, including doses delivering bromate exposures within an order of magnitude of what high consumers of bromated bread might receive over a lifetime.

**Umemura 1998 (PMID 9630011)** exposed male and female F344 rats to KBrO3 in drinking water at 60, 125, 250, and 500 mg/L for 13 weeks and measured 8-hydroxydeoxyguanosine in kidney DNA at multiple time points. Male rats showed rapid 8-OHdG elevation that persisted throughout the experiment, accompanied by sustained proximal tubule cell proliferation. Female rats showed delayed but eventually significant elevation. Critically, the 8-OHdG response correlated with the eventual tumor sites — kidney proximal tubule — and not with non-target organs, providing a clean mechanistic link between oxidative DNA damage and renal carcinogenesis. The authors concluded that persistent elevation of 8-OHdG, not transient bursts, drives tumor development.

**Umemura and Kurokawa 2006 (PMID 16473452)** addressed the human-relevance question directly. Some chemical carcinogens that cause kidney tumors in male rats do so through accumulation of α-2u-globulin, a protein found only in male rats — an effect that is irrelevant to humans. The authors examined whether bromate's renal carcinogenicity might be α-2u-mediated and concluded it was not. Renal tumors occur in both male and female rats and the underlying mechanism is oxidative stress, which operates in human kidney tissue as well. They concluded that bromate "must be considered carcinogenic" for human risk assessment.

**International Agency for Research on Cancer (Monograph Volume 73, 1999) [7]** integrated the animal carcinogenicity data, mechanism studies, and limited human exposure data and classified potassium bromate as Group 2B — possibly carcinogenic to humans. The classification was based on sufficient evidence of carcinogenicity in experimental animals (kidney, thyroid, peritoneum) combined with a relevant mechanism (oxidative DNA damage producing 8-OHdG) operating across species. JECFA, the joint FAO/WHO expert committee, came to a similar conclusion and withdrew its acceptable daily intake.

**Islam 2024 (PMID 39619963)** measured potassium bromate in 104 baked food samples collected across Bangladesh. Bread samples ranged from below detection limit to 5.53 mg/kg — up to 276 times the FDA's 0.02 µg/g residue limit. Cake samples reached 6.18 mg/kg (309× the FDA limit). Burger buns ranged from 0.86 to 1.69 mg/kg, pizza crusts from 0.92 to 4.35 mg/kg, and naan up to 5.37 mg/kg. The data demonstrate that real-world post-baking residues are not consistently below safety limits and that the assumption underlying the FDA's continued tolerance — that proper baking destroys all the bromate — is not reliable in practice.

**Ayembilla 2024 (PMID 39734470)** sampled 285 bread loaves across all ten regions of Ghana, where potassium bromate is officially banned. Despite the ban, 12.3% of samples (35 of 285) tested positive for KBrO3 at concentrations of 0.46-26.67 µg/g — 23 to 1,334 times above the FDA limit. The authors calculated dietary hazard quotients of 0.166 to 0.435, all below the threshold of 1.0 that would indicate definite excess cancer risk at typical Ghanaian bread consumption levels, but only because the average daily bread intake was modest. The persistence of bromate even in countries where it is officially banned suggests that enforcement is the limiting factor and that voluntary FDA guidance in the United States is unlikely to eliminate exposure.

**California AB 418 (2023) [8]** is not a research paper but a legislative response to this body of evidence. California's Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment first listed potassium bromate as a Proposition 65 carcinogen in 1990. The 2023 California Food Safety Act takes the additional step of banning manufacture, sale, or distribution of food containing potassium bromate (along with brominated vegetable oil, propylparaben, and Red Dye No. 3) starting January 1, 2027. The legislative findings cite the IARC classification, the consistent animal evidence, and the regulatory consensus in Europe and elsewhere as the basis for the ban.

**Strength of the evidence and limitations.** The animal carcinogenicity data are unusually strong: multiple species, multiple sexes, multiple target organs, dose-response relationships at exposures within an order of magnitude of estimated high-end human dietary intake, and a well-characterized oxidative-stress mechanism with direct human relevance. There are no large epidemiological cohort studies in humans because bromate exposure has been declining in most countries for decades, making prospective human studies impractical. The ecological evidence — that countries banning bromate have not seen population-wide health effects, while countries permitting it cannot rule out modest excess kidney cancer risk in heavy bread consumers — is consistent with a real but small lifetime cancer risk at typical Western dietary exposure. For an individual consumer, the practical takeaway is that avoidance is straightforward, alternatives produce equally good bread, and there is no nutritional or culinary downside to choosing unbromated flour.
