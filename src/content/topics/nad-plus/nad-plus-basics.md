NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a molecule found in every cell of your body that acts as a fundamental fuel for cellular energy production, DNA repair, and longevity signaling. The problem is that NAD+ levels decline significantly with age — roughly 50% by middle age — and this decline is now recognized as a key driver of the metabolic slowdown and increased disease risk that come with aging [1]. Supplements like NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NR (nicotinamide riboside) are precursors that the body converts into NAD+, and human clinical trials now show they can meaningfully raise NAD+ levels in blood and muscle [4][7].

## How NAD+ Works

NAD+ plays two essential roles in human biology: it powers your mitochondria and activates your longevity proteins.

### Cellular Energy Production

In your mitochondria, NAD+ acts as an electron carrier in the process that converts glucose and fats into ATP — the energy currency your cells run on. Without adequate NAD+, this process becomes less efficient. This is why low NAD+ is associated with fatigue, reduced physical capacity, and metabolic sluggishness. Every cell in your body depends on it, but tissues with high energy demands — muscle, brain, heart — feel the impact of decline most acutely.

### Sirtuins: Your Longevity Proteins

NAD+ is the essential fuel for a family of proteins called sirtuins (SIRT1–SIRT7). Sirtuins regulate some of the most important processes in aging biology: DNA damage repair, inflammation control, mitochondrial quality, and cellular stress responses [1][2]. When NAD+ is abundant, sirtuins are active and protective. When NAD+ falls — as it does with age — sirtuin activity decreases, and the hallmarks of aging accelerate: accumulating DNA damage, chronic low-grade inflammation, declining mitochondrial function [3].

### DNA Repair Enzymes (PARPs)

NAD+ is also consumed by PARP enzymes that repair DNA strand breaks. As we accumulate more DNA damage with age, PARPs become more active — competing with sirtuins for the same pool of NAD+. This creates a vicious cycle: more damage → more PARP activity → less NAD+ → less sirtuin activity → more damage.

### The Age-Related Decline

Research shows NAD+ drops approximately 50% from young adulthood to middle age, and continues falling. This is due to several factors: decreased production via the NAD+ salvage pathway, increased consumption by PARPs and inflammatory enzymes (CD38), and reduced efficiency of the enzymes that recycle NAD+ [1]. Restoring NAD+ through precursor supplementation is the logic behind NMN and NR.

## NMN vs. NR: What's the Difference?

Both NMN and NR are NAD+ precursors — molecules the body converts into NAD+. They're both forms of vitamin B3 (niacin) derivatives, but they differ in their biochemical pathway and some practical aspects:

- **NR (Nicotinamide Riboside):** Discovered earlier; more early human research available. Must be converted to NMN first before becoming NAD+. Absorbed orally and raises blood NAD+ dose-dependently [4].
- **NMN (Nicotinamide Mononucleotide):** One step closer to NAD+ in the biosynthesis pathway. Has strong human RCT data for muscle insulin sensitivity, blood NAD+ elevation, and physical function [6][7][8].
- **Dose ranges:** Most human trials use 250–1,000 mg/day for NMN or NR. A multicenter dose-finding trial found optimal effects at 600 mg/day NMN for NAD+ levels and physical performance markers [7].
- **Both are well-tolerated** with no significant safety signals in trials up to 12 weeks at these doses.

## Practical Considerations

- **Who might benefit most:** People over 40, those with metabolic conditions, people experiencing fatigue or reduced exercise capacity
- **Food sources of NAD+ precursors:** Niacin (B3) from meat, fish, and legumes provides some precursors, but not in the concentrated amounts used in research trials; edamame contains NMN in small amounts
- **Timing:** Most people take supplements in the morning since NAD+ is involved in energy metabolism
- **Combination considerations:** NAD+ supplements are often combined with resveratrol (activates SIRT1 differently), and pterostilbene (a resveratrol analogue) has been studied alongside NR
- **Storage:** NMN can degrade with heat and light; look for sublingual or encapsulated forms stored properly

## Evidence Review

### Foundational Mechanistic Research

The 2014 review by Imai and Guarente (PMID 24786309), two of the leading researchers in NAD+ biology, established the conceptual framework: NAD+ levels fall substantially with aging across multiple tissues in both rodents and humans, and this decline impairs sirtuin activity, which in turn accelerates multiple aging phenotypes including metabolic dysfunction, inflammatory dysregulation, and mitochondrial decline. This paper remains the canonical reference for understanding why NAD+ decline matters.

Mouchiroud et al. (PMID 23870130), published in *Cell* in 2013, demonstrated in *C. elegans* that restoring NAD+ through supplementation activated sirtuin signaling, triggered mitochondrial stress responses via the unfolded protein response (UPR^mt), and extended lifespan. While animal data, this study established the mechanistic chain from NAD+ → sirtuins → longevity that underlies the entire field.

Cantó and Auwerx (PMID 22106091) provided a comprehensive mechanistic review of how NAD+ controls SIRT1 activity, which then deacetylates PGC-1α — the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. This pathway connects NAD+ to mitochondrial number and quality, providing a mechanistic basis for why NAD+ decline affects physical capacity.

### Human NR Trials

Trammell et al. (PMID 27721479), published in *Nature Communications* in 2016, was the landmark first human trial of NR. In 12 healthy adults, a single dose of 1,000 mg NR raised whole-blood NAD+ by up to 2.7-fold within hours, with smaller but significant increases at 300 mg. This established that NR is orally bioavailable and effectively raises the NAD+ metabolome in humans. The study identified NAAD (nicotinic acid adenine dinucleotide) as a sensitive biomarker of NAD+ repletion.

Elhassan et al. (PMID 31412242), published in *Cell Reports* in 2019, conducted a placebo-controlled crossover RCT in 12 aged men using 1 g NR/day for 21 days. NR significantly elevated the NAD+ metabolome in skeletal muscle and induced anti-inflammatory transcriptomic signatures, with downregulation of inflammatory gene programs. This is particularly relevant given that muscle is a primary site of age-related metabolic decline. Sample size was small (n=12), which is a significant limitation.

### Human NMN Trials

Yoshino et al. (PMID 33888596), published in *Science* in 2021, is arguably the strongest human NMN trial to date. In a 10-week double-blind RCT of 25 postmenopausal prediabetic women (250 mg/day NMN), the NMN group showed a 25% improvement in insulin-stimulated muscle glucose disposal as measured by hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp — the gold standard for measuring insulin sensitivity. NMN also upregulated expression of genes involved in muscle remodeling and glucose transport (including GLUT4). This is the first demonstration that NMN produces meaningful metabolic benefit in humans, not just NAD+ biomarker changes.

Yi et al. (PMID 36482258), published in *GeroScience* in 2023, was a multicenter double-blind RCT in 80 healthy middle-aged adults randomized to 300, 600, or 900 mg/day NMN or placebo for 60 days. All NMN groups showed significant increases in blood NAD+ compared to placebo. The 600 mg/day group showed the best combination of NAD+ elevation and physical performance improvements. Importantly, all doses were well-tolerated with no significant safety signals — providing the best available dose-safety data in humans to date.

Igarashi et al. (PMID 35927255), published in *NPJ Aging* in 2022, conducted a double-blind RCT in healthy older men receiving NMN supplementation. NMN significantly raised whole-blood NAD+ and showed changes in muscle function metrics over the treatment period. This study from Imai's group (a leading NMN researcher with partial commercial conflicts of interest worth noting) adds to the growing human evidence base.

### Overall Evidence Assessment

The human evidence for NAD+ precursor supplementation is more robust than for most longevity-focused supplements. Multiple double-blind RCTs confirm that NMN and NR reliably raise NAD+ levels in blood and muscle at doses of 250–1,000 mg/day, with good tolerability. The most clinically meaningful human finding is improved muscle insulin sensitivity with NMN (Yoshino et al.), which is a hard metabolic endpoint rather than just a biomarker shift.

Limitations to keep in mind: most human trials are small (12–80 participants), short (8–12 weeks), and not always designed to detect hard clinical endpoints like disease incidence or mortality. The mechanistic rationale is strong, but translating biomarker improvements into long-term health outcomes remains to be demonstrated in larger, longer trials. The field also has notable conflicts of interest, as several leading researchers have commercial ties to supplement companies. The evidence justifies cautious optimism — this is one of the more scientifically grounded areas of longevity supplementation — but it would be an overstatement to claim proven anti-aging effects in humans at this stage.
